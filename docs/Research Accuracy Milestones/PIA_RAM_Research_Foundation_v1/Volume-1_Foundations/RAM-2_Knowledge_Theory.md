# 1. Introduction

The previous chapter established the complete observable information space of software engineering.

However, observations themselves are not the objective of PIA.

Organizations do not seek commits.

Organizations do not seek pull requests.

Organizations do not seek issue comments.

Instead, organizations seek answers to questions such as:

* Who understands this system?
* Who should own this module?
* What knowledge is at risk?
* Who can replace the current maintainer?
* How resilient is this organization?

None of these quantities are directly observable.

This chapter introduces the concept of **Latent Organizational Knowledge**, the hidden variable that PIA ultimately seeks to estimate.

---

# 2. Motivation

Current repository mining approaches generally assume the following process:

```text
Commit
      ↓
Metric
      ↓
Expertise
```

This assumption is fundamentally flawed.

A commit is merely an observable action.

Expertise is an internal organizational property.

The two cannot be considered equivalent.

Therefore PIA rejects the assumption that activity directly measures expertise.

---

# 3. Research Question

The primary research question of RAM-2 is:

> **What hidden quantity are software engineering events actually revealing?**

Answering this question is essential because every future algorithm depends upon the nature of the hidden variable.

---

# 4. Observable Versus Latent Variables

Software engineering consists of two fundamentally different categories of variables.

## Observable Variables

Observable variables are directly measurable.

Examples include:

* commits
* reviews
* deployments
* documentation edits
* issue discussions
* build executions
* incident responses

These variables are visible to external systems.

---

## Latent Variables

Latent variables cannot be directly observed.

Examples include:

* knowledge
* expertise
* familiarity
* ownership tendency
* architectural understanding
* debugging capability

These variables exist inside the organization but cannot be measured directly.

---

# 5. Fundamental Observation

A commit does not contain knowledge.

Instead,

a commit is evidence that knowledge may exist.

Likewise,

a review does not equal expertise.

It merely provides evidence that expertise may exist.

Therefore,

software engineering events are observations rather than organizational intelligence.

---

# 6. Hidden Organizational Knowledge

PIA introduces the concept of Hidden Organizational Knowledge.

Let

K

represent the true organizational knowledge possessed by an individual regarding a software artifact.

K is never directly observed.

Instead,

PIA observes engineering events generated by K.

Consequently,

knowledge must be inferred rather than measured.

---

# 7. Why Knowledge Is Latent

Consider three developers.

Developer A designed the architecture five years ago.

Developer B maintains the system daily.

Developer C reviews every pull request.

Who possesses the greatest expertise?

No observable metric can answer this question directly.

The answer depends upon hidden organizational knowledge.

This demonstrates that expertise is fundamentally a latent variable.

---

# 8. Problems With Existing Expertise Scores

Traditional expertise metrics generally rely upon:

* commit count
* changed lines
* ownership percentage
* file touches

These metrics suffer from several limitations.

Large refactoring may produce thousands of modified lines without increasing knowledge.

Generated code inflates activity while contributing little understanding.

Documentation work may transfer enormous knowledge despite modifying little source code.

Consequently,

activity-based metrics frequently misrepresent organizational knowledge.

---

# 9. Research Hypothesis

RAM-2 introduces the following hypothesis.

> **Software engineering expertise is a latent organizational variable that cannot be directly measured from engineering activity.**

Instead,

observable engineering events provide probabilistic evidence regarding the hidden knowledge state.

---

# 10. Mathematical Interpretation

Let

Y

represent observable software engineering events.

Let

K

represent latent organizational knowledge.

The objective of PIA becomes

P(K | Y)

This probability expresses the belief that hidden organizational knowledge exists given observed engineering activity.

Every later algorithm within PIA attempts to improve this estimate.

# 12. Research Contributions

RAM-2 establishes four key principles.

1.

Knowledge is not directly observable.

2.

Engineering events constitute observations rather than organizational intelligence.

3.

Expertise must be inferred rather than measured.

4.

Repository metrics are evidence, not truth.

# 13. Limitations

The current chapter deliberately avoids defining the internal structure of knowledge.

Knowledge is treated only as a hidden variable.

The next milestone addresses the question:

> **Is knowledge a single scalar quantity, or does it possess internal structure?**
